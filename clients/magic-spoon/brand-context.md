## Brand Overview

Magic Spoon is a direct-to-consumer better-for-you breakfast brand that reimagines classic American childhood cereals — Frosted, Cocoa, Fruity, Peanut Butter, Cinnamon — with adult-optimized nutrition: high protein (13–14g per serving), 0–2g sugar, keto-friendly, and gluten-free. Founded by Greg Sewitz and Gabi Lewis, the brand sits at the intersection of nostalgia and modern health-consciousness, targeting millennials and Gen Z adults who grew up loving sugary cereal but now want food that performs for their lifestyle. Magic Spoon sells exclusively DTC at a premium price point ($9–$10+ per box) and has expanded beyond cereal into granola, cereal treats, and pastries.

---

## Brand Story & Origin

Greg Sewitz and Gabi Lewis met in college, lived together, and previously co-founded another food company together (Exo, a cricket protein bar company). Both grew up eating cereal every morning and described the experience as: love the taste, hate the crash. As adults, they searched for a cereal that had the same "addictive quality" of childhood classics but actually fueled them — and found nothing.

Their discovery: the average American eats 100 bowls of cereal a year, yet almost every option in the grocery aisle is "stuck in that old paradigm of grains and sugar." They spent over a year experimenting to create a cereal "inspired by the flavors and nostalgia of Saturday-morning-cartoon cereal but upgraded for a 21st-century consumer." Magic Spoon launched in 2019 as a DTC brand and quickly became a podcast advertising darling — most notably with Joe Rogan's endorsement on The Joe Rogan Experience. The founders describe the brand's mission simply: a guilt-free treat that tastes like you remember and you can eat at any time of day.

## What Makes Them Different

Magic Spoon's core differentiator is the combination of **childhood flavor nostalgia + adult nutritional performance** — in a single product that doesn't require compromise. Their own words: *"a cereal inspired by the flavors and nostalgia of Saturday-morning-cartoon cereal but upgraded for a 21st-century consumer."*

**Unique mechanism / nutritional stack:**
- **High protein:** ~13–14g per serving (vs. 2–4g in traditional cereal)
- **0–2g sugar** (vs. 10–15g+ in comparable classic cereals)
- **Keto-friendly** macros (low net carbs)
- **Gluten-free**
- Achieves this through a proprietary blend of milk protein isolate and alternative sweeteners (monk fruit, allulose), rather than grain-based carbohydrate bases

**The key creative insight:** Competitors in "healthy cereal" tend to look and taste like health food. Magic Spoon tastes like the cereal you loved as a kid — the box art, the flavors (Frosted, Cocoa, Fruity), the marshmallow bits — but delivers a macro profile that would have seemed impossible in that format. This is the core "magic."

**Proof points used in messaging:**
- 80,000+ 5-star reviews (confirmed from promo bar)
- "Happiness 100% Guaranteed" (confirmed from site)
- Joe Rogan podcast endorsement (user-provided, confirmed in brand lore)
- "Childhood Classics. Grown-Up Ingredients." — official homepage headline (confirmed)

## The Alternative Solution

**The "old way" this replaces:**

1. **Traditional sugary cereal** — eating Frosted Flakes, Lucky Charms, or Cocoa Puffs with the pleasure of nostalgia but accepting the sugar crash, carb load, and guilt. This is the primary "old way" — something most of the audience literally grew up doing.

2. **Giving up cereal entirely** — Health-conscious adults who loved cereal as kids have largely abandoned the category. They switched to eggs, protein shakes, Greek yogurt, or overnight oats. Magic Spoon's pitch to this group is: *you don't have to give up cereal, we rebuilt it for you.*

3. **"Health-adjacent" cereals that don't taste good** — Products like plain granola, bran-forward cereals, or protein-added cereals that sacrifice flavor for nutrition. Magic Spoon explicitly calls out that it tastes like you remember — the indulgent eating experience is preserved.

## Core Audience(s)

**Primary:** Millennial adults (primarily 25–40), health-conscious, who grew up eating sugary cereal and feel genuine nostalgia for it but have since shifted to better nutritional habits. They track macros or follow a keto/low-carb diet, care about protein intake, and are willing to pay premium prices for food that fits their lifestyle. Fitness and wellness-oriented. Often busy, want convenient nutrition.

**Secondary A:** Keto/low-carb community — people in active dietary restriction who miss the full range of "normal" food experiences. Cereal is one of the things they've had to give up; Magic Spoon solves a real hole in that diet.

**Secondary B:** Parents who want to eat something sweet and satisfying without undermining their health goals — and may also want an option that's lower-sugar for household use.

**Secondary C:** Podcast listeners and JRE fans who first discovered Magic Spoon through host-read ads — solution-aware consumers who know the product exists but haven't converted.

**Key audience signals:**
- Pain points: Sugar crash from old cereal, giving up childhood favorites to eat healthy, boredom with "health food" that tastes like cardboard, wanting something that actually tastes good
- Values: Nutrition optimization, body composition, active lifestyle, nostalgia, permission to enjoy food
- Language patterns: "Feels like cheating," "I can't believe this is healthy," "tastes exactly like [childhood cereal]," "actually hits my macros"
- Price point acceptance: Willing to spend $9–10+/box when justified by macro performance

## Brand Voice & Tone

**Adjectives:** Playful, nostalgic, confident, direct, slightly cheeky, approachable, clean.

**Patterns:**
- Leads with the emotional/nostalgic benefit first, then backs it with the nutritional fact. Never clinical first.
- Short, punchy headlines: *"Childhood Classics. Grown-Up Ingredients."* / *"Happiness 100% Guaranteed"*
- Leans into the word "cereal" unapologetically — doesn't rebrand itself as a "protein snack" even though it competes there
- Uses nostalgia as permission: "guilt-free treat that tastes like you remember"
- Reviews lean heavily on surprise/disbelief: *"I can't believe this is [macro profile]"* — the brand amplifies this
- Casual, first-person founder voice on the story page: *"Hi, we're Greg and Gabi"* — warm and relatable, not corporate
- CTAs are action-forward but fun: *"Build your own bundle →"* vs. generic "Shop Now"
- Certified gluten-free is surfaced in reviews and product language for that sub-audience

**What they avoid:**
- Overly clinical nutrition-speak (they're not a supplement brand)
- Diet culture shame language
- Anything that makes it feel like "health food" rather than real food
- Medical claims (confirmed constraint)

A significant portion of the existing customer base first heard of Magic Spoon this way. This creates a "solution aware" base who may need a retargeting push more than an awareness push.

- **Product line has expanded significantly.** They've moved from a single cereal SKU into Treats, Granola, and now Pastries. This is both an upsell/cross-sell opportunity and a targeting complexity — different product lines likely appeal to different sub-segments of the audience.

- **Seasonal/limited products active.** "Spring Granola" featured in promo bar as limited edition — they do seasonal releases, which creates urgency creative opportunities.

- **"FREE Bowl Set for New Subscribers"** — active acquisition offer for new subscribers visible in promo bar. This is likely a live creative hook to leverage.

- **Gluten sensitivity is a real, mentioned driver** — one featured review specifically calls out gluten sensitivity as a purchase reason. This is an underserved targeting angle alongside keto.

- **"Cereal entrepreneurs"** — founders explicitly use this pun on the About page. Brand has a sense of humor about itself.
