# Strategy Matrix: Saved by Grace Co. (SBG Co.)

*1 persona(s) × 5 Schwartz awareness stages = 5 messaging cells*

---

## The Faithful Boy Mama *(primary)*

### Unaware

**Angle:** Interrupt her scroll with the mirror she didn't know she needed — show her what it looks like when a boy mom's wardrobe actually matches her faith and her family.

**What they know:** She knows she's a boy mom and she loves Jesus. She buys apparel occasionally but hasn't thought critically about the gap between what she's wearing and what she actually believes about her identity. She scrolls Instagram, double-taps matching family photos, and quietly wishes her wardrobe reflected her life more honestly — but she hasn't named this as a problem yet.

**Gap to fill:** That there is a brand built specifically for her — a faith-forward, boy-mama-specific, woman-of-color-owned label that was designed to fill the exact gap between 'Sunday best' and 'everyday mom uniform.' She doesn't know SBG Co. exists, and she doesn't know she's been settling.

**Hook style:** pattern-interrupt / aspirational visual stop

**Example hook:** *"Nobody told me there was a shirt made for moms who love Jesus AND root for their wild little boys every single day."*

**Framework:** `bab` | **Mechanic:** Trojan Horse — opens as a relatable 'boy mom life' moment (muddy cleats, loud house, boys hanging off her) and reveals the apparel organically as part of the scene, not as a product demo. The brand enters through the emotion, not the pitch.

**Proof to surface:** Founder story — Whitney Vaughn, woman of color, built this from nothing in North Carolina since 2016. The brand IS the story.

**CTA:** See the collection | **Funnel:** cold

*Notes: Do not lead with product features or price. Lead with the life. The 'Wild Like My Curls' and 'Kicks Over Chicks' visual language can anchor this without needing to explain either product — let curiosity do the work. Representation matters here: showing a Black or mixed-race mom-and-son moment in the creative is both authentic to the brand and a meaningful pattern interrupt in a space that skews white.*

---

### Problem Aware

**Angle:** Name her frustration out loud — validate that the gap is real, that she's not too picky, and that settling for soulless Amazon tees is not the only option.

**What they know:** She knows she wants to dress herself and her boys in something that feels like them — faith-adjacent, boy-energy-appropriate, not stiff or preachy. She's looked. She's found the Christian bookstore section and felt nothing. She's scrolled Etsy and bought something that arrived looking different from the photo. She has a vague, unresolved frustration: nothing out there feels both beautiful and right.

**Gap to fill:** That the problem isn't her taste — it's the market. Most Christian apparel is either built for a 60-year-old church lady or mass-produced by someone who has never actually raised a boy. She doesn't yet know SBG Co. exists as the answer to exactly this complaint.

**Hook style:** empathy-opener / pain-callout

**Example hook:** *"I spent way too long buying 'boy mom' shirts that had zero faith and Christian shirts that had zero personality. I was looking for both and couldn't find either — until this."*

**Framework:** `pas` | **Mechanic:** Talking Head Confession — founder or relatable mom-creator speaks directly to camera, naming the exact frustration in plain language before pivoting to the discovery moment. Feels like a text from a friend, not an ad.

**Proof to surface:** Featured on the Today Show — use this as the signal that this brand is not another Etsy shop. It's the real thing that actually got national recognition for doing what the market wasn't doing.

**CTA:** Explore SBG Co. | **Funnel:** cold

*Notes: This is the highest-leverage cold stage for this brand because the audience's pain is specific and nameable. The creative should feel like a sigh of relief — 'finally, someone gets it.' Avoid any creative that feels like a comparison ad at this stage; the goal is validation, not competition. The Today Show mention earns credibility without requiring the customer to already trust the brand.*

### Solution Aware

**Angle:** Position SBG Co. not as another option in the category but as the brand that was built for her — when everything else is either mass-produced or misaligned, SBG Co. is the one that actually sees the boy-mama faith identity.

**What they know:** She knows brands like SBG Co. exist — she may have seen a reel, a friend's post, or a mention. She's also aware of the Etsy-made-to-order category and Amazon's boy-mom section. She's in evaluation mode: is there a brand that does faith + boy-mom identity + real quality + a real story? She's comparing options, even loosely.

**Gap to fill:** That SBG Co. is categorically different from both Etsy and Amazon in ways that matter to her — it's made to order in North Carolina (not overseas), it's woman-of-color-owned (not a faceless brand), and it's been nationally featured (not just well-tagged on Instagram). She doesn't yet know that the brand she's vaguely aware of is actually the specific answer she's been looking for.

**Hook style:** differentiation frame / 'there's a better way' opener

**Example hook:** *"Amazon has the boy mom shirts. Etsy has the custom sets. But neither of them was made by a woman of color in North Carolina who built her whole brand around raising boys with faith. That's a different thing entirely."*

**Framework:** `fab` | **Mechanic:** Contrast Without Comment — side-by-side or sequential comparison of the 'alternatives' (generic, mass-produced, faceless) versus SBG Co. (specific, made-to-order, story-driven). Let the contrast do the persuading without editorializing. Ends on an SBG Co. product moment — no narration needed.

**Proof to surface:** Made to order in North Carolina, USA + woman-of-color founder since 2016 + Today Show feature. Stack all three as the differentiation proof. These are not marketing claims — they are facts the competition cannot replicate.

**CTA:** Shop the Boy Mama Collection | **Funnel:** cold/warm

*Notes: This stage should do real positioning work — not just vibes. The creative must make SBG Co.'s differentiators feel tangible, not aspirational. 'Made to order' should be tied to quality and intentionality, not just process. 'Woman-of-color-owned' should be shown (founder imagery, Whitney's face), not just stated. The Mamas Dude Checkered Tee and Kicks Over Chicks Sweatshirt are the right hero products to feature here — both speak directly to the boy-mama niche that distinguishes SBG Co. from generic Christian lifestyle brands.*

### Product Aware

**Angle:** Collapse her objections one by one with social proof, size guidance, and framing that recontextualizes 'made to order' from a risk into a reason to buy.

**What they know:** She knows SBG Co. She may have visited the site, saved an Instagram post, or been tagged in a friend's photo. She's seen the Kicks Over Chicks Sweatshirt or the Mamas Dude Checkered Tee. She's interested — maybe even close. But she's sitting on objections: the 14-day turnaround feels risky if she has an event coming, the price premium feels hard to justify compared to Amazon, or she's been burned by small shop sizing before and can't easily return a made-to-order item.

**Gap to fill:** That the 14-day turnaround is a feature, not a bug — it means her item is being made specifically for her, not pulled off a shelf of generic inventory. She also doesn't know how many moms in her exact situation have ordered and loved it, or that the brand's quality is consistent enough to have earned national press.

**Hook style:** objection-handling / social-proof anchor

**Example hook:** *"I was nervous about the two-week wait and the no-return policy on a made-to-order shirt. Then it arrived. Worth every single day."*

**Framework:** `pastor` | **Mechanic:** Social Witness — real customer UGC or creator review that walks through the objection journey: 'I almost didn't order because...' followed by the reveal moment when the product arrived. The emotional payoff (unboxing, wearing it, the photo moment with her son) is the conversion proof.

**Proof to surface:** Customer reviews and UGC — specifically any that reference quality, sizing accuracy, or made-to-order experience. Today Show feature as ambient trust signal. If available, surface specific customer quotes about the matching mom-and-son moment or the way the product photographed.

**CTA:** Order yours — allow 14 days for your made-to-order piece | **Funnel:** warm/retargeting

*Notes: The 14-day turnaround is the single biggest conversion objection for this brand and must be addressed proactively — not defensively. Frame it as craftsmanship, not delay. The custom quote sign (top SKU) and the Mamas Dude Tee are both made-to-order and have the same objection surface area, so creative built for this stage can work across both product types. Do not use urgency tactics that feel artificial — this audience is faith-aligned and values authenticity over pressure. Instead, use occasion-based urgency: 'Easter is 6 weeks out. Made-to-order takes 14 days. Order now to wear it in time.'*

### Most Aware

**Angle:** Give her the final push with a direct, warm, occasion-anchored CTA that treats her purchase as an expression of her identity, not just a transaction.

**What they know:** She's ready. She's seen the product, she knows the brand, she's resolved her objections (or nearly so). She may be sitting in an abandoned cart, re-visiting the product page, or waiting for a nudge — a discount, a deadline, a final reason to click. She needs permission and a clear path forward.

**Gap to fill:** That this is the exact right moment to order — whether because a seasonal window is closing, because she's already been thinking about it for weeks, or because someone she trusts just told her to stop waiting. She may also not know if a bundle or occasion tie-in exists (e.g., matching mom-and-son Easter set) that would make the decision feel even more justified.

**Hook style:** direct close / occasion urgency / identity-confirmation

**Example hook:** *"You've been thinking about it. The Mamas Dude Tee, the Kicks Over Chicks Sweatshirt for your son, the matching photo you've been planning. Made to order in North Carolina. Here's your sign — order today."*

**Framework:** `slap` | **Mechanic:** Implied Answer — the creative opens on the emotion she already has (the photo she wants, the moment she's imagining) and the product is the obvious, already-understood answer. No explanation needed. Just confirmation that she was right to want it.

**Proof to surface:** Customer photo (UGC) of a mom and son actually wearing the matching pieces — the image IS the proof. Support with the Today Show credibility badge and any urgency-relevant timing signal (seasonal deadline, processing time reminder).

**CTA:** Order now — made to order, ships in 14 days | **Funnel:** retargeting

*Notes: This audience does not need more information — they need confidence and a clear path. Keep the copy tight. Lead with the emotional payoff image (the matching moment, the photo, the church outfit), not with product specs. For abandoned cart sequences specifically, a warm, non-salesy tone consistent with the brand voice is critical — do not pivot to high-pressure language. A line like 'We saved your cart. Whenever you're ready, we'll make it for you.' is more on-brand than a countdown timer. If a seasonal window is relevant (Easter, 4th of July, back to school), the 14-day lead time becomes a natural, non-manipulative urgency driver.*

## Cross-stage observations

**Highest leverage stages:** problem_aware — The pain is specific, nameable, and widely felt among the target persona. The market gap (faith + boy-mom identity + real quality) is real and demonstrable. Creative that names this frustration clearly will stop the scroll and earn trust before asking for anything., product_aware — This is where purchases are being lost. The 14-day made-to-order turnaround is the primary conversion friction point. Creative that proactively collapses this objection with real customer social proof will have outsized impact on conversion rate relative to spend., most_aware — The brand has a warm audience of past customers and engaged followers who are ready to buy again or complete a purchase they've been considering. Retargeting with occasion-anchored urgency (Easter, summer, back to school) is a high-ROI play given that this audience already trusts the brand.

**Weakest stages:** unaware — Cold reach is expensive and the brand's awareness is still building outside of its existing community. Without a large organic reach or influencer amplification, paid unaware content requires budget that may be better allocated to problem_aware and product_aware stages first., solution_aware — The comparison positioning requires the creative to do more intellectual work and risks feeling like it's punching at competitors. This stage is valuable but needs careful execution to avoid sounding defensive rather than confident.

**Budget/creative distribution:** For current brand scale, recommend a weighted distribution of approximately 40% problem_aware, 30% product_aware/retargeting, 15% solution_aware, 10% most_aware retargeting, and 5% unaware. As the brand scales budget and builds a larger cold audience through organic and influencer channels, shift investment toward unaware (target 20-25%) to grow the top of funnel with personas who don't yet know SBG Co. exists. The most_aware retargeting budget should flex up seasonally (Easter, 4th of July, holiday) when occasion-based urgency makes the 14-day turnaround a conversion driver rather than a deterrent.

**Category notes:** Faith-forward apparel for moms operates on a trust-first buying psychology — this audience is deeply attuned to whether a brand is genuinely faith-aligned or using Christianity as an aesthetic. Every creative asset should feel like it was made by someone who actually believes what the brand says, not someone who researched the niche. Whitney Vaughn's face and founder story are among the brand's most powerful creative assets and should appear in paid content across all stages — not just the about page. The made-to-order model is simultaneously the brand's strongest quality signal and its biggest conversion barrier; every stage of the funnel should be doing some version of reframing this as craftsmanship rather than inconvenience. Seasonal moments (Easter, 4th of July, back to school, Christmas) are the brand's natural purchase windows and should anchor the most_aware and product_aware creative calendars. The Custom Quote sign (top SKU by volume) is underrepresented in this persona's awareness journey — it is a high-intent gift and home decor purchase that warrants its own funnel treatment separate from the apparel products featured here.
