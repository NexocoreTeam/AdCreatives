## Brand Overview

Saved by Grace Co. (SBG Co.) is a faith-forward apparel and lifestyle brand founded in 2016 by Whitney Vaughn, a woman of color, and made to order in North Carolina, USA. The brand creates Christian clothing, kids' apparel, home décor, and accessories designed for faith-driven moms and their children — with a strong emphasis on the boy-mom identity, matching family moments, and wearing your values without apology. Featured on the Today Show, SBG Co. occupies a distinct position between mass-market Christian bookstore apparel and generic Etsy matching sets — offering the warmth of a small, woman-owned business with the polish of a nationally recognized brand.

## Brand Story & Origin

SBG Co. was founded in 2016 by Whitney Vaughn, a woman-of-color entrepreneur based in North Carolina. The brand was built around the belief that faith should be wearable, beautiful, and woven into everyday life — not reserved for Sunday mornings. Whitney started the brand as a small, made-to-order operation and has grown it into a nationally recognized name in the Christian lifestyle space, earning a feature on the Today Show. The name "Saved by Grace" is itself a declaration of identity — not just a brand name, but a statement of belief that functions as the brand's north star.

The brand's origin is rooted in the intersection of personal faith, motherhood, and the desire to dress your family in things that mean something. Whitney's identity as a woman of color and a woman-owned business founder is part of the brand's story and should be treated as an asset in creative — authentic representation in a space that skews heavily white.

## What Makes Them Different

SBG Co. sits at the intersection of **faith, identity, and motherhood** — a combination that mass-market competitors don't authentically own. Their differentiation stacks across several dimensions:

1. **Made to order in North Carolina** — Every item is handcrafted domestically, not mass-produced overseas. This is a confirmed brand claim displayed prominently on the homepage: *"Each item is made to order, ensuring quality. Made in North Carolina, USA."*

2. **Woman of color-owned, since 2016** — In a space dominated by white-led brands, Whitney Vaughn's identity as a Black woman founder is both authentic representation and a point of genuine differentiation. This is especially relevant for the "Wild Like My Curls" product line and any multicultural audience targeting.

3. **National recognition** — The Today Show feature is prominently displayed in the announcement bar and homepage trust icons. This is a credibility anchor that Etsy sellers and Amazon listings categorically cannot claim.

4. **Faith-forward without being preachy** — SBG Co. integrates belief into everyday apparel in a way that feels like identity expression rather than evangelism. The products speak the language of the buyer's values, not a sermon.

5. **Boy-mama identity niche** — While the brand serves all moms and kids, the "Kicks Over Chicks," "Feral," and "Mamas Dude" franchise products speak directly to a boy-mom identity that is underserved in the broader Christian lifestyle market.

6. **Aesthetic quality** — The brand's visual identity (see Voice & Tone section) is closer to Magnolia or Madewell than to a church gift shop, making it aspirational rather than merely devotional.

## Must-Know Strategic Context

**The Boy Mama Identity is the core unlock.**
The boy-mom identity is deeply sentimental and underserved in the faith apparel space. These women don't just want a tee — they want something that says *I see you, I celebrate you, and your specific kind of motherhood matters.* The "Kicks Over Chicks" + "Feral" + "Mamas Dude" franchise is SBG Co.'s strongest differentiator in this category. Creative for the Boy Mama Collection should lean into the emotion of raising boys, not just the product itself.

**Matching moments are a purchase trigger.**
The audience values mom-and-kid matching as a form of love language. "Mamas Dude" as a companion to boys' items signals this. Ad creative that shows a mom and son in coordinated pieces will outperform product-only shots for this audience.

**The Today Show feature is a gold-tier trust anchor.**
This is the #1 credibility signal in the brand's arsenal. It should appear in ad creative wherever possible — it immediately separates SBG Co. from every Etsy seller and Amazon listing in the category.

**Made to order = premium signal, not a liability.**
The 14-day turnaround is a potential objection but also a quality signal. "Made to order" in North Carolina reads as artisan, intentional, and worth waiting for — if framed correctly. Don't hide it; reframe it as craftsmanship.

**Whitney Vaughn's identity is an asset.**
Woman of color, woman-owned, since 2016 — this is a founder story that resonates deeply with a community that genuinely cares about who they're buying from. The story should be told in creative, not buried in an About page.

**The brand has significant catalog breadth — focus matters.**
800+ SKUs means the brand can feel overwhelming in ads. For the Boy Mama Collection, creative should hero a tight cluster of products (Kicks Over Chicks franchise, Feral, Mamas Dude) rather than try to show the full range.

**Seasonal and patriotic collections are recurring revenue drivers.**
"Loves Jesus & America Too" and "All American Boys Club/Mamas Club" are consistent top performers. 4th of July is likely a peak revenue moment. Holiday-adjacent creative deserves dedicated strategy.

**Visual identity should drive production briefs.**
The Gemini visual analysis is authoritative for this brand: earthy/garment-dyed tones, sun-drenched lifestyle photography, neutral staging (shiplap, sand, cream), short all-caps sans-serif phrases, comfort-colors-style blanks. Any UGC or produced creative should match this aesthetic exactly. Off-brand visuals (harsh lighting, bright saturated colors, loud backgrounds) will feel jarring to this audience.
